# Researcher Quantum Impact

## Overview

The Researcher Quantum Impact module addresses the unique cryptographic challenges faced by academic researchers, data scientists, and interdisciplinary scientists whose work involves long-lived sensitive datasets, collaborative data sharing, federated infrastructure, and emerging PQC research directions. It covers six high-impact research-specific concerns — long-lived confidential data exposure, academic publishing integrity, HPC and cloud research infrastructure, current PQC cryptanalysis research, cross-disciplinary PQC applications, and emerging grant funding requirements — alongside a nine-criterion research self-assessment and a Research Agenda Planner. Content is calibrated for researchers who must think about both protecting their own data and contributing to or consuming PQC knowledge.

## Key Concepts

- **Long-Lived Research Data Exposure (HNDL)** — genomic and biomedical data has confidentiality lifetimes of 75+ years (patient privacy, research embargoes, competitive advantage); clinical trial data under FDA 21 CFR Part 11 must be preserved for 15+ years post-study; 20-year grant archives with proprietary pre-publication findings; any data encrypted today with RSA-2048 or ECDH and stored for 10+ years is an HNDL target — a CRQC arriving in 2030–2035 can retroactively decrypt it; data classification by HNDL risk must precede migration prioritization
- **Genomic Data Protection** — human genome sequences: ~3 GB per individual, typical study cohort 10,000–100,000 subjects; encryption at-rest with AES-256-GCM + ML-KEM-1024 KEK is the recommended pattern (AES-256 is quantum-resistant for confidentiality; ML-KEM replaces ECDH for key encapsulation); re-identification risk from genomic data makes it effectively permanent PII — the confidentiality lifetime extends to the subject's lifetime plus statutory retention periods; GA4GH (Global Alliance for Genomics and Health) Beacon v2 and Passports APIs require authentication; PQC JWT signing required as these APIs evolve
- **Academic Publishing and Preprint Integrity** — DOI-linked publications rely on digital signatures for long-term authenticity verification; preprint servers (arXiv, bioRxiv, SSRN) do not currently sign submissions; co-author identity verification uses email-based or ORCID-based authentication — both need PQC upgrade paths; timestamp and non-repudiation proofs for priority claims (patent disputes, Nobel attribution) may need to survive 30–50 years; ML-DSA-87 or SLH-DSA-SHAKE-256s are the recommended algorithms for long-lived document signing
- **HPC and Cloud Research Infrastructure** — HPC cluster authentication: Kerberos v5 + SSH host keys; ML-DSA SSH host key migration requires coordinated `known_hosts` updates across all submitter nodes; MPI job scheduler (SLURM, PBS Pro): TLS connections for job submission APIs; cloud burst computing (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud research programs): TLS and IAM credential exposure during burst periods extends HNDL surface; multi-institutional data sharing via Globus or rsync over SSH requires both endpoints to support PQC before the link is secure
- **Lattice-Based Cryptography Research** — the hardness of ML-KEM and ML-DSA rests on the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem and its variants Module-LWE (MLWE) and Ring-LWE (RLWE); the Short Integer Solution (SIS) problem underpins ML-DSA signatures; current best lattice attacks (BKZ algorithm, core-SVP hardness) show no polynomial-time quantum speedup; researchers in lattice cryptanalysis, algebraic geometry, and number theory are the most relevant contributors to PQC security proofs; parameter selection in NIST finalists reflects conservative estimates of BKZ attack cost at quantum security levels (ML-KEM-768: NIST Level 3 = 128-bit quantum security)
- **Code-Based and Multivariate Cryptography** — code-based schemes (Classic McEliece, HQC, BIKE) rely on hardness of decoding random linear codes; McEliece has 50+ years of cryptanalysis history with no successful attack; large public key sizes (McEliece-8192128: 1 MB public key) limit adoption; multivariate polynomial systems (UOV, Rainbow, MAYO) offer small signatures but have had multiple breaks — Rainbow was broken in 2022; researchers should treat multivariate schemes as research-grade, not production-grade
- **Cross-Disciplinary PQC Applications** — biology + cryptography: DNA storage encryption (ML-KEM for key encapsulation over DNA-encoded data), genomic co-computation via FHE (Zama TFHE, Microsoft SEAL); physics + cryptography: QKD integration with PQC (hybrid QKD+ML-KEM for defense channels), quantum random number generation (QRNG) for entropy sourcing (ID Quantique, Toshiba QRNG); mathematics + cryptography: isogeny-based schemes (SIKE was broken in 2022; SQISign remains a research candidate); chemistry + cryptography: molecular simulation data protection (long-lived IP)
- **Quantum Random Number Generators (QRNG)** — NIST SP 800-90A/B/C define deterministic (DRBG) and non-deterministic (entropy source) RNG requirements; QRNG devices (ID Quantique Quantis, Toshiba TRBG, Cambridge Quantum IronBridge) provide NIST SP 800-90B-compliant entropy; hybrid entropy mixing (QRNG + system entropy) is recommended over QRNG-only to guard against QRNG hardware failures; FIPS 140-3 Level 3 validation is available for select QRNG modules; researchers studying entropy analysis should understand health tests (repetition count test, adaptive proportion test per SP 800-90B §4)
- **Federal Grant Funding PQC Requirements** — NIH: no explicit PQC mandate as of March 2026, but data management plans for genomic data increasingly reference NIST cybersecurity framework; NSF: SaTC (Secure and Trustworthy Cyberspace) program actively funds PQC research; EU Horizon Europe: PQC requirements emerging in Digital Europe Programme calls; DARPA PQMS (Post-Quantum Microelectronics Security) program; DoD SBIR/STTR: explicit PQC requirements for information assurance categories; researchers submitting to these programs should include PQC data protection plans in their DMP sections

## Workshop / Interactive Activities

The workshop has 3 interactive steps:

1. **Threat Impact Explorer** — six-panel research-context briefing: HNDL exposure calculator for research data (input: data type + confidentiality lifetime → output: HNDL risk tier and recommended algorithm), academic publishing integrity analysis (DOI signature lifetime vs. algorithm security lifetime, preprint signing options), HPC infrastructure threat map (Kerberos + SSH host key migration complexity, Globus endpoint PQC requirements), PQC research landscape navigator (lattice/code-based/hash-based/multivariate comparative analysis with current cryptanalysis status), cross-disciplinary application matrix (biology/physics/chemistry research data protection patterns with PQC recommendations), and grant funding PQC requirements tracker (NIH/NSF/EU Horizon/DARPA current requirements and emerging mandates)
2. **Self-Assessment Survey** — nine-criterion research readiness evaluation covering: handles confidential research data with >5 year retention (genomic, clinical, proprietary), has HPC cluster access (SLURM/PBS/LSF job submission via SSH or TLS), participates in multi-institutional data sharing agreements (Globus, DMP-mandated sharing), uses academic publishing workflows (preprint submission, DOI registration, co-author verification), conducts cryptography research (lattice cryptanalysis, protocol design, implementation), receives funding from federal agencies with PQC-relevant data requirements (NIH/NSF/DoD/EU Horizon), has long data retention requirements under regulatory or institutional policy, engages in international research collaborations with data export controls (ITAR, EAR, GDPR cross-border), maintains pre-publication embargo periods (>12 months); outputs a research data risk tier with prioritized protective actions
3. **Research Agenda Planner** — three-section structured planning tool: (1) Data Protection Plan — classifies each research data category by HNDL risk tier, maps to recommended PQC algorithm and key management approach, generates a DMP-compatible PQC section for grant applications; (2) Infrastructure Migration Checklist — HPC host key migration steps, Globus endpoint PQC upgrade timeline, cloud research environment TLS configuration, collaborative data sharing protocol updates; (3) Research Direction Map — for cryptography researchers: current open problems in lattice hardness (MLWE parameter tightness, NTRU variants, algebraic structure attacks), hash-based signatures (XMSS parameter optimization, SLH-DSA performance analysis and parameter selection), entropy analysis (QRNG health test design, SP 800-90B compliance testing); exports as structured Markdown suitable for inclusion in grant proposals

- NIST SP 800-90C (Recommendation for Random Bit Generator Constructions — draft)

- GA4GH Framework for Responsible Sharing of Genomic and Health-Related Data
