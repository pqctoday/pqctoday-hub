The aerospace industry manages the extremely sensitive communication between planes in the sky, satellites in space, and air traffic control on the ground. These communications rely on very strict, certified security protocols that are mathematically locked tight. Because planes and satellites are built to last for decades, the security we install in them today must still be protecting them in the year 2050.

Because these vehicles live for so long, the current encryption protecting their communications will eventually become vulnerable to future quantum computers. A hacker could potentially record instructions sent to a satellite today and decode them years later. Worse, because you can't easily swap out the physical computer chips on a satellite already floating in space, the industry is racing against a ticking clock.

To prepare, aerospace engineers are testing new, high-tech Post-Quantum encryption methods. However, these new methods are often mathematically "heavier" and require more bandwidth. Engineers must figure out how to squeeze this massive new security into older radio networks without disrupting the split-second timing required to keep planes flying safely.
